On Writing Well in the LLM Age

[ 2026-07-08 ] — Brendan Tuckerman

"Writing is related to character. If your values are sound your writing will be sound. It all begins with intention. Figure out what you want to do and how you want to do it, and work your way with humanity and integrity to the completed article."

-- William Zinsser 'On Writing Well'[^1].

I had fallen out of love with writing--mostly because I was so bad at it.

There was a time where I aspired to be a writer; the trouble was I 'knew' I could never be one. There were plenty of excuses: I didn't have the right stuff to be a writer (whatever that stuff was supposed to be), I didn't have enough life experience, I didn't have the right pedigree. I had fallen into the trap of believing that the ability to write well was something you either had or you didn't, rather than the result of hard work and repeated practice.

Reading 'On Writing Well' recently helped me to reflect on what I had so wrong about writing.

That I somehow missed reading William Zinsser's 'On Writing Well' for so long is a surprise--it sold over a million copies and is compared to E.B. White's classic 'Elements of Style'. But maybe certain books come into your life when you are ready for them. My younger self would have likely ignored the advice and denounced its prescriptivism: don't try to restrict my free expression with your stuffy rules!

I'm glad I finally read it; the advice spoke to me. Write simply. Write honestly. Edit ruthlessly. Write for the audience of yourself. All things that I had been failing to do. Unpretentious, funny and clear, 'On Writing Well' reminded me of the humanity and skill that makes good writing. What struck me most is Zinsser's view of writing an honest, deeply personal act of self-expression:

"[T]he purposes that writers serve must be their own. What you write is yours and noone elses. [...] Writing well means believing in your writing and believing in yourself, taking risks, daring to be different, pushing yourself to excel."

Equally, 'few people realize how badly they write', he says in a statement that could have been written for me alone. I don' think I've written a line that hasn't caused me to cringe in embarrassment, but 'On Writing Well' encourages us that through practice we can improve.

I wonder what Zinsser would make, then, of the increasing role of LLMs in this human endeavour. If we choose, we can do all our writing through a short prompt. More and more of us are choosing to do this. A study of 300 million documents in 2024 found that almost a quarter of business press releases were AI generated[^2]; a number which is likely to be higher today. Anyone who uses the internet is well aware of the endless blog posts, articles, posts and entire websites generated through AI. Most of which suck.

With the ease of writing in this way, the role of human-as-writer is under threat. Research into which jobs are most at risk of automation puts professional writers near the top of the list[^3], alongside clerical workers and translators. While I am skeptical of the ability for LLMs to replace (good) writers, I am less skeptical that CEOs believe that LLMs can do a job that looks like a writer, thus saving them a buck. Thus, more and more of the writing we read will be produced by LLMs and more of the writing we produce will be spat out by an AI assistant--if only to keep up with new productivity expectations.

As 'Writing Well' points out--writing is hard. It requires thought and time. It requires "[w]riters [to] constantly ask: what am I trying to say? Surprisingly, often they don't know." Particularly for the more mundane types of writing (think emails and meeting minutes), who could blame people for wanting to use them? Because this outsourcing is cognitively easy, we run the risk of collectively diminishing our ability to express ourselves. Writing as a craft is an easy thing to lose.

Slop grenades and boring ouroboros.

"Clear thinking becomes clear writing; one can't exist without the other."

If writing is 'thinking on paper', then who is doing the thinking for us when we ask an LLM to write an email or an article or even a whole novel[^4]? And when writing has been produced by a machine because it was too much effort to write, why would I put in the effort to read it? There is already resistance to reading the output from LLMs, with tell-tale signs like emoji-ridden lists and overly verbose responses. Those who cut and paste unedited LLM responses into emails and chats have been accused of lobbing 'slop grenades' at their colleagues[^5].

There are implications for novelty, creativity and diversity. At their core, LLMs are statistical probability machines. That is, they predict what the most likely next word(s) will be based on the contextual information provided; like a sophisticated autocomplete. One study which analysed 2,200 college level essays to compare how novel the ideas were when compared to those produced by LLMs discovered a paradox. On an individual level, LLMs introduced more novel ideas than most people. But when compared across a group there was less diversity, which became less and less diverse with each AI generated essay that was included[^6]. Most notably, the study found that the 'models are fundamentally trained to conform to existing data distributions that are biased in favor of predominant cultural ideas'. The information-dense, verbose syntax and grammar of AI responses defaults to an 'Exam English'[^7], so our collective voice becomes that of an annoying know-it-all, reinforcing existing dominant cultural values.

So when we look out at the sea of online writing, we risk seeing a boring ouroboros-- homogeneous writing trained on homogeneous writing--eating its own tail as it continues to train itself on insipid data.

The solution is good writing.

Good writing not only breaks rules, it shapes them. It is the deviation from the norm that makes 'good' writers good--while LLMs pull us back towards the norm like crabs in a bucket.

The slow (brain) food movement

"Banality is the enemy of good writing; the challenge is to not write like everyone else."

In the 1980s, the 'Slow Food' movement arose as a response to the global spread of fast food. It promotes 'good, clean and fair food' over unhealthy, environmentally unsustainable, and ubiquitous alternatives. I propose the same for writing. A movement that eschews using LLMs in writing to keep Zinsser's writing-as-a-craft alive. A movement that embraces the effort needed to write even a half-decent sentence.

There are more pragmatic reasons to want to be able to write well. If everyone else is producing slop, then the person who can express themselves with a unique voice stands out and has value to an employer. And people who can explain why writing is good are useful in being able to guide others to do the same.

As someone who uses LLMs daily, I don't want to dismiss their use entirely. There just hasn't been a technology to which we could outsource our own self-expression so easily. We need to recognise that the voice of ChatGPT or Gemini or Claude is not our own.

While I am still a bad writer, I'm working on it. Slowly. Through practice, in an effort to express myself better each day.

And there is something uniquely human about that.
